Introduction

Introduction & Description of Data

Given that content recommendation systems are becoming a big issue for the users and also for the companies who provide these services (Netflix paid out $1,000,000 in prize money for the winners of the Netflix competition[4] ) there is scope to delve into this field and be the ones to make the next big leap forward. Personally we’ve also experienced the lackluster feeling when Spotify recommendations become monotonous and strongly feel like this field could benefit from something more human and varied.

In this report we investigate the million playlist dataset, provided by Spotify, containing a collection of one million user generated playlists and the collection of songs within them. Our aim is to use this data to generate an automatic recommendation system, one which can be given any collection of songs as a “seed playlist” and then generate a series of recommendations (potentially near infinite although theoretically limited by number of unique songs) to be added to this playlist. This approach is designed to be scalable - want to create a brand new 100 song playlist from three songs? - specify a three song seed and request the top 100 recommendations. Wish to add a few new songs to an existing 50 song playlist? - the approach is the same. In a sense we’ve tried to combine both the cold start and playlist continuation into a single framework - given a series of songs of an arbitrary length, what are the n most relevant songs.

This however brings up the concept of relevance - what is a relevant song for a playlist? Within the genre of recommender systems we wish to push our system beyond the simple mathematical representation of songs (the “characteristics” which can be described through the frequencies of the waveform such as tempo, pitch and key). Whilst useful metrics of song similarity in some situations (see ordering later on in this paper) these metrics cannot fully capture what makes a playlist a playlist. Our theoretical assumption is that playlists are uniquely human, that they are more than just a collection of similar songs by some simple metrics but are instead a hidden narrative. A simple example of this would be a Christmas playlist - whilst they all share a common theme (lyrics about snow and Santa) they are musically diverse spanning classical, pop, rock, country and a whole host of other genres and styles. In order to replicate this sort of diversity it is vital for us to generate an understanding of this “hidden dimension” of the data.

Challenges - Data Size and Scope

One of the major practical challenges of the Spotify million playlist data is it’s sheer size. Each playlist can range in length from a few, to a few hundred songs giving us tens if not hundreds of millions of songs. Each of these songs have a wealth of meta-data available on the Spotify API of which we could leverage as much or as little as possible. Just the simple song to playlist csvs are approximately 11GBs of data and this is prior to attaching any of the song characteristics data. The first step of this project will be to come up with a methodology for storage and processing of this data in an efficient manner.

Challenges - Benchmarking and Comparisons

The biggest conceptual challenge in this project is one of benchmarking and evaluation. Given that we are attempting to recommend songs and playlists in a manner which is more varied and human than simply recommending songs with similar characteristics it doesn’t necessarily make sense to use these same characteristics to score how well the models are performing. Without a mathematical representation of fitness - a loss function - training traditional supervised machine learning models such as regression and supervised neural networks becomes problematic. Manual inspection can be used to some extent to ensure the model are recommending songs which we know to be similar in some regard but this is impractical when considering and comparing multiple models.

What we propose is that there is that framed in a specific manner, the song characteristics can still be a valid scoring metric. Simply put we wish to recreate a playlist in the manner a human would, recommending interesting and varied songs which are connected by some complex thread. So to benchmark how well a model is performing we can leverage these pre-specified human created playlists. Removing a portion of songs and then predicting new songs to replace them should allow us to directly compare these two sets. Whilst we wouldn’t expect the songs to be identical (there may be a huge variation of potentially valid songs) we will assume that the songs characteristics of the original, user selected, songs were chosen because they in some way “complete” the playlist. Therefore the closer the model suggested songs are to the originals the better the model is in terms of its ability to make playlists in a “human” manner. We are aware that this is an approximation but it should still allow for relative model comparison and scoring.

Data Queries

Before commencing with the modelling phase we first delve into the dataset itself to understand and draw out the key information which will help guide the approach. There are a number of questions we’d like to answer about the data before committing to a modelling approach and broadly these break down into two categories:

  1. What do the song characteristics look like?
  2. What does the playlist and song space look like?
