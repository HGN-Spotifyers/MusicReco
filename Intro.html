Introduction

Introduction & Description of Data

Given that content recommendation systems are becoming a big issue for the users and also for the companies who provide these services (Netflix paid out $1,000,000 in prize money for the winners of the Netflix competition[4] ) there is scope to delve into this field and be the ones to make the next big leap forward. Personally we’ve also experienced the lackluster feeling when Spotify recommendations become monotonous and strongly feel like this field could benefit from something more human and varied.

In this report we investigate the million playlist dataset, provided by Spotify, containing a collection of one million user generated playlists and the collection of songs within them. Our aim is to use this data to generate an automatic recommendation system, one which can be given any collection of songs as a “seed playlist” and then generate a series of recommendations (potentially near infinite although theoretically limited by number of unique songs) to be added to this playlist. This approach is designed to be scalable - want to create a brand new 100 song playlist from three songs? - specify a three song seed and request the top 100 recommendations. Wish to add a few new songs to an existing 50 song playlist? - the approach is the same. In a sense we’ve tried to combine both the cold start and playlist continuation into a single framework - given a series of songs of an arbitrary length, what are the n most relevant songs.

This however brings up the concept of relevance - what is a relevant song for a playlist? Within the genre of recommender systems we wish to push our system beyond the simple mathematical representation of songs (the “characteristics” which can be described through the frequencies of the waveform such as tempo, pitch and key). Whilst useful metrics of song similarity in some situations (see ordering later on in this paper) these metrics cannot fully capture what makes a playlist a playlist. Our theoretical assumption is that playlists are uniquely human, that they are more than just a collection of similar songs by some simple metrics but are instead a hidden narrative. A simple example of this would be a Christmas playlist - whilst they all share a common theme (lyrics about snow and Santa) they are musically diverse spanning classical, pop, rock, country and a whole host of other genres and styles. In order to replicate this sort of diversity it is vital for us to generate an understanding of this “hidden dimension” of the data.
