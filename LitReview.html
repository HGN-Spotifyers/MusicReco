Literature Review

Literature Review on Recommender Systems

As entertainment companies and media outlets turn to online distribution methods, data-driven recommender systems have become increasingly important. They help platforms make decisions about what type of content to produce and how to distribute it, and also assist users in sifting through a deluge of options to find the content that interests them most.

Furthermore, the rise of the digital economy provides new opportunities to collect data about consumer preferences and use them to influence purchasing habits. Seeing potential revenue gains, a number of companies have invested time and money into improving their recommender systems, mostly famously in 2006, when Netflix put up a $1 million USD prize for the team that proposed the best method for improving their predictions of how a user would rate a given film [3]. Recommender systems therefore remain an active area of research. The current literature on these systems focuses on a number of key areas.

Content-Based Recommendations vs. Collaborative Filtering

Hu et al. (2008) provide a useful distinction between content-based recommender systems and collaborative filtering approaches:

The content based approach creates a profile for each user or product to characterize its nature. As an example, a movie profile could include attributes regarding its genre, the participating actors, its box office popularity, etc. [...] Collaborative Filtering analyzes relationships between users and interdependencies among products, in order to identify new user-item associations [4].

For example, collaborative filtering seeks to identify “pairs of items that tend to be rated similarly or like-minded users with similar history of rating or purchasing” [4].

Spotify data has been the subject of many recommender system studies. For example, Pérez-Marcos et al. (2018) propose a method that relies on a Pearson’s coefficient to suggests songs for a user to listen to based on a comparison of their playback history with the overall play count across all Spotify users.

During the Netflix Prize, non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) was developed as an alternative to coefficient-based approaches. NMF relies on decomposing a matrix of user-item ratings into two factor matrices, one which characterizes users and one which characterizes items, such that the product of the two factors reconstructs the original matrix with minimal error. This provides a way of predicting the rating a each user would give to each item. The majority of the leading entries in the Netflix Prize competition uses some form of ensemble method based on NMF [6].

A good music recommendation is about more than the song itself – it depends on both the individual preferences of the user and the context in which they are listening to the song. We will therefore focus on recommender systems that provide personalized and user-dependent outputs, leading us to favor a collaborative filtering approach. As a baseline, we will also consider a content-based model with recommends songs based on their attributes (tempo, valence, etc.) without taking into account any data on playlist structure.

Explicit vs. Implicit Rating

As pointed out by Hu et al. (2008), user feedback is not always provided explicitly [4]. In some cases, information about a user’s interest in a particular item can be gleaned from behavioral data collected by an online platform. For example, Netflix may deduce that a user is not interested in a TV series if they stop watching after the pilot episode.

They successfully design a TV show recommender system that relies solely based on implicit feedback data collected from 300,000 TV set-top boxes, indicating which users tuned in to 17,000 unique programs which aired during a four week period. One of the main challenges they identify in working with this sort of data is that it becomes more difficult to evaluate the confidence of the predictions: in contrast to a system where users directly report preferences (such as a 1 to 5 star rating), it is not always clear when observed represents a strong or weak preference.

In addition to the distinction between implicit and explicit preferences, work on recommender systems also considers situations with missing or one-sided data. Steck (2010) focuses on methodologies for training and testing recommender systems on data missing not at random [8]. Verstrepen et al. (2014; 2017) study the problem of making recommendations with only positive data, which occurs for example when the data indicates that a user liked a particular item but no information about when they did not like it (i.e. the model does not distinguish negative and neutral preferences) [9] [10].

One-way data collection is an important consideration for playlist recommendation systems: the fact that a user chose to include a given song in their playlist is a strong positive signal that the song goes well with the others in the playlist; but the corresponding negative signals about all the songs that they did not include may not be as strong: perhaps there are other songs that they happened not to include, but may still have been fitting choices. In our approach, we will consider weighting positive events more strongly than negative events to reflect the fact that our dataset is biased toward positive data.

Single vs. Multiple Recommendations

A number of papers have focused on the task of recommending a slate of items rather than an individual item, such as a group of items that a user may want together [7] [11] [1]. Deshpande et al. (2004) point out that user-based collaborative filtering methods can become extremely computationally intensive as the number of users increase, and suggest alternative approaches focusing instead on user–item matrices. They propose a class of “top-n recommendation” algorithms that focus on similarities between the items themselves, and then use user–item matrices and then uses them to identify the set of items to be recommended” [2].

We believe that song recommendations are best characterized as a group of items rather than individual items. We will therefore pursue an approach that learns from the structure of playlist data to propose groups of songs that pair well together. This enables recommendations to be context-dependent. For example, a user’s interest in a particular song may depend on which other songs they listen to in a particular session. The basic unit of our recommender system will therefore be a group of songs.

Incorporating User Feedback

The interaction between users and platforms gives rise to a number of interesting feedback loops. Jiang et al (2019) discuss on feedback loops in recommender systems [5]. Viappiani et al. (2006) propose a framework for eliciting user preferences in web searches using a multi-stage process. When users enter a search query, they are presented with initial suggestions. They are then given options to revise their search in response to these suggestions. The algorithm can take into account both what was previously known about the user’s preferences and the new information collected from their response to the suggestions. [12]

In the case of our Spotify recommender system, there is currently no mechanism for soliciting user feedback about our recommendations. However, feedback loops remain important to keep in mind as they offer a helpful lens for thinking about the social and temporal dynamics of music preferences, since past recommendations may influence future preferences. For example, songs that are recommended to numerous users may, by virtue of that recommendation, become more popular; this in turn may increase other users’ interests in those songs.
